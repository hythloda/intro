Featured App Coupon Guidance

AS OF 06.30.26

Featured App Reward Coupons exist to align Reward Weight with real economic contribution to the network.  They can be produced by either Featured CC Transfers or Featured App Markers; both approaches produce $1 of reward weight per use.
They are not a mechanism to amplify rewards beyond underlying on-chain activity.

Each marker represents $1 of reward weight.  Reward weight is not the same as the ultimate reward amount paid out.  It is expected that the ratio of reward paid to reward weight will vary both above and below 1:1, but this does not affect the amount of reward weight an app may create.

Failure to adhere to this guidance may result in immediate pause of Featured App (FA) status, potentially without warning, while the Tokenomics Committee reviews usage.

Core Rules

1. No Redundant Markers on CC Transactions

Both markers and featured CC transfers generate coupons.  The number of coupons created must be in line with this guidance regardless of whether they are generated by markers or featured CC transfers.

CC transactions already include a provider field that attributes activity appropriately.
Adding markers to CC transactions would result in double counting of reward weight and distort Burn/Mint equilibrium.

Note: If an app’s traffic costs average <$1 per CC transfer, they can unfeature some of those transfers by removing the Featured App Contract ID from the Transfer Context.  Alternatively, applications may unfeature all of their transfers and use markers instead, or some mix of the two.

CIP-0107 has been deployed on mainnet, allowing featured CC transactions to create markers on-chain rather than directly creating coupons. This simplifies the calculations present in this guidance.

2. Coupon-to-Fee Alignment (Default Rule)

Featured Apps may not generate more coupon reward weight, via either CC transfers or marker submissions, than the net on-chain fees they generate.  Apps may receive a free traffic allowance from the synchronizer; to account for this, while keeping calculations simple, each featured app is credited for 0.1 MB of traffic per round in addition to the traffic they purchase.

Rule:
 1 marker or featured CC transfer = $1 of reward weight
Maximum markers = net qualifying on-chain fees (USD)

Example

If an FA generates:

  • $10 in qualifying fees → maximum 10 markers

Qualifying fees exclude any fees associated with the markers themselves.

Monitoring

The query under monitoring will be used to monitor and measure a Featured App’s Marker to Fee Ratio.

Precision Tolerance

Because of rounding, batching, and timing differences:

  • Variance within ±15% is acceptable by default.
  • Variance greater than 15% requires explanation.
  • Persistent or unexplained divergence may trigger review.

This rule preserves Burn/Mint equilibrium and prevents marker inflation relative to actual economic burn.

3. No Markers for the Cost of Submitting Markers

Featured Apps may not include the cost of submitting markers when calculating allowable markers.

Markers cannot justify themselves.

Example

If:

  • Total fees generated = $10
  • $3 of that is marker submission cost

Then:

  • Net eligible fees = $7
  • Maximum allowable markers = 7

This prevents circular farming and self-reinforcing reward loops.

4. Markers Only for On-Chain Fee-Generating Activity

Markers may only be submitted for actions that directly result in on-chain traffic fees.

Markers are not allowed for:

  • Reading ledger data
  • Off-chain API calls
  • UI interactions
  • Off-chain matching
  • Any activity that does not generate on-chain burn

If no on-chain fee is generated, no marker may be submitted.

5. Timeliness Requirement (Two-Round Rule)

Markers must be submitted within two reward rounds of the fees they correspond to.

If an FA misses this window, the opportunity to earn rewards for that activity is forfeited.

Submitting markers long after fees were generated:

  • Alters the FA reward distribution profile
  • Distorts expectations for other participants
  • Introduces instability into reward forecasting

Markers are intended to reflect contemporaneous economic activity.

6. No Net-Paying Users for Activity

Featured Apps may not use rewards to net pay users for activity.

Permitted:

  • Offsetting legitimate user costs
  • Reducing fees
  • Supporting real usage

Not permitted:

  • Paying users more than their costs
  • Buying artificial transaction volume
  • Creating circular activity designed primarily to farm rewards

Markers must reflect organic demand, not purchased activity.

7. No Reward Recycling

Featured Apps may not earn rewards from distributing rewards to users.

You may not:

  • Submit markers on transactions whose primary purpose is distributing previously earned rewards.
  • Create recursive reward loops.

Rewards cannot generate new reward weight.

8. Asset Issuers

The default rule (1 marker per $1 of fees generated) assumes the Featured App directly incurs on-chain fees.

However, asset issuers often do not directly submit the transactions that use their asset. As a result, their direct fee bucket may be $0, even when their asset is widely used on-chain.

If held strictly to the default rule, asset issuers could be entitled to zero markers and zero rewards despite meaningful on-ledger usage.

Therefore:

Asset issuers are permitted to submit markers for on-ledger activity involving their asset, even if they incur no direct fees for that activity.

Asset Issuers may mark transactions submitted by 3rd party venues (or exempt 1st party venues) in the follow way:

  • min ( 50% of transaction cost , $1 )

Example 1:

  • A DVP costs ~ $2
  • The venue marks $2
  • AssetA marks $1
  • AssetB marks $1

Example 2:

  • An intra-wallet transfer costs ~ $0.53
  • The wallet marks $0.53
  • The asset marks $0.265

Conditions

  • The activity must generate on-chain fees (even if paid by another party).
  • The asset must be genuinely used in the transaction.
  • Markers must correspond to objective, verifiable on-chain events.
  • Asset use markers must not be applied when used within your own app or venue
  • All other rules (no farming, no reward recycling, no off-chain marking, timeliness requirement) still apply.

What this does not allow

  • Marking off-chain activity
  • Creating artificial transactions to farm rewards
  • Embedding usage fees solely to manufacture a fee bucket
  • Marking the use of your own asset within your app or venue

Principle

For standard FAs → eligibility is based on fees generated.
For asset issuers → eligibility is based on on-ledger asset usage (excluding their own venue).

This exception ensures asset issuers are not structurally excluded from rewards simply because they do not directly bear submission costs, while still maintaining alignment with real economic activity. Preventing the marking of assets in your own venue encourages use and composability of assets across the ecosystem.

Monitoring

The  query under monitoring will be used to monitor and measure a Featured App’s Marker to Transaction Ratio.

Exceptions

It is expected there may be extenuating circumstances that would merit an exception to Internal vs External Asset marking rules. These exceptions will be considered on a case-by-case basis by the Tokenomics Committee.

Forward Guidance

We recognize that with any new guidance there is the potential for misuse of the Featured App designation and markers to emerge. It is important to address this proactively but take sufficient time to provide thoughtful and  thorough guidance. To that end, there will likely be further fair use guidance on specific asset issuer use cases. Topic to be considered include but aren't limited to wrapping of already on Canton assets, wrap/unwrap loops, and transaction sharding.

9. CIP-0056 Compliance

In order to be eligible for FA Rewards, assets must be fully CIP-0056 compliant.

Compliance

Asset Issuers must demonstrate ability for users to hold assets in at least 2 wallets live in the ecosystem with CIP-0056 support.

Asset Issuers must demonstrate ability for users to swap assets via at least one on-chain DVP facility.

10. Separate Party Concerns

Where a Featured App operates multiple materially distinct functions, those functions should be separated into distinct on-chain Parties.

This requirement is mandatory for asset issuers.

General Rule

If an application performs more than one major role — for example:

  • Asset issuance
  • Wallet services
  • DEX functionality
  • Custody
  • Lending
  • Payment processing

Each major function should operate under a separate PartyId.

Reward weight, fee generation, and marker submission must be attributable to the correct Party corresponding to the economic role being performed.

Asset Issuers – Mandatory Separation

If an entity acts as an asset issuer, that PartyId may not also perform other major functions.

Specifically, an Asset Issuer PartyId may not simultaneously operate as:

  • A wallet
  • A DEX
  • A market maker
  • A lending protocol
  • A payment processor
  • Any other fee-generating application function

The Asset Issuer must be isolated from other operational roles.

Rationale

This requirement prevents:

  • Blending issuer-based marker privileges with fee-based privileges
  • Cross-subsidization of reward weight
  • Obscured economic attribution
  • Difficulty in auditing burn-to-marker alignment

Asset Issuer treatment is already an exception to the default marker rule. That exception requires structural clarity to preserve integrity.

Enforcement

Failure to adhere to any of the above guidance may result in:

  • Immediate pause of Featured App status
  • Pause without prior warning
  • Review by the Tokenomics Committee
  • Potential revocation of Featured App status

The objective is to preserve:

  • Burn/Mint equilibrium
  • Fair reward attribution
  • Stability of reward distribution
  • Long-term tokenomic integrity

-- This query relies on a set of four tables

-- 1. mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_scan_verdict_store

-- 2. mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_scan_verdict_transaction_view_store

-- 3. mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_update_history_creates

-- 4. mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_update_history_exercises

-- These tables are just document stores containing the corresponding elements from the public

-- Scan API. The fields accessed throughout are fairly evident on the Scan API.

-- The only non-API field used here is the rowId on verdicts, which is used to link the verdict

-- and view tables.

with

-- We start pulling the data for the asset guidance: Successful transactions confirmed

-- by the confirmer-party and whether they were submitted by the own validator(s)

-- or third parties.

-- Start with pulling verdicts, which will allow us to measure successful transactions

-- and whether they were self-submitted or not.

 verdicts as (

   select

     TIMESTAMP_BUCKET(timestamp_micros(record_time), bucket_width, bucket_origin_timestamp) as bucket,

     row_id as verdict_row_id,   -- Used to link views

     update_id,                  -- Used to correlate with other tables

     verdict_result,             -- Used to filter only successful transactions

     submitting_participant_uid, -- Used to determine whether the transaction was self-submitted.

   from `mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_scan_verdict_store`

 ),

-- Next we get our hands on views to figure out whether the provider party was a confirmer. This is

-- needed for the asset type measurements.

 views as (

   select

     verdict_row_id,

     -- A bit of JSON mangling here to get the confirmers into a list of unique confirmers.

     ( SELECT ARRAY_AGG(distinct flat_confirmers)

       FROM (

         SELECT ARRAY_CONCAT_AGG(ps) cs

         FROM (

           select JSON_VALUE_ARRAY(confirmer_groups, '$.parties') ps

           FROM UNNEST(JSON_QUERY_ARRAY(confirming_parties)) as confirmer_groups

         )

       ), UNNEST(cs) as flat_confirmers

     ) as confirmers

   from `mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_scan_verdict_transaction_view_store`

 ),

 -- Now we annotate which transactions were confirmed by the confirmer party,

 -- and which transactions were submitted by the validator parties.

 -- This allows us to measure the asset guidance later.

 annotated_views as (

   select *,

   -- Was it confirmed by the provider party?

   ( select COUNT(*)

     from unnest(confirmers) as cs

     where cs in unnest(confirmer_parties)

   ) > 0 as confirmer_confirmed

   from views

 ),

 -- Now join views with verdicts

 annotated_verdicts as (

   select

     bucket,

     update_id,

     -- Was the transaction self-submitted?

     submitting_participant_uid in unnest(validator_parties) as validator_submitted,

     -- If any view was provider confirmed, then so was the verdict.

     countif(confirmer_confirmed) > 0 as confirmer_confirmed,

   from verdicts

   left join annotated_views using(verdict_row_id)

   where bucket >= bucket_origin_timestamp

     and verdict_result = 1   -- Only count successful transactions

   GROUP BY bucket, update_id, validator_submitted

 ),

 grouped_verdicts as (

   select

     bucket,

     countif (confirmer_confirmed) as confirmer_confirmed,

     countif (validator_submitted) as validator_submitted,

     -- This last one is "qualifying" for assets

     countif (confirmer_confirmed and not validator_submitted) as third_party_submitted

   from annotated_verdicts

   group by bucket

 ),

 -- Now look at burn. Burn can be read from templates of type `MemberTraffic`.

   burn as (

   select

     update_id,

     record_time,

     TIMESTAMP_BUCKET(timestamp_micros(record_time), bucket_width, bucket_origin_timestamp) as bucket,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[0].value.party') as dso,

     -- Note here that we filter out the "PAR::" prefix.

     substring(JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[1].value.text'), 6) as member_id,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[2].value.text') as synchronizer_id,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[3].value.int64') as migration_id,

     LAX_INT64(JSON_QUERY(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[4].value.int64')) as traffic,

     LAX_INT64(JSON_QUERY(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[5].value.int64')) as num_purchases,

     LAX_FLOAT64(JSON_QUERY(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[6].value.numeric')) as amulet_spent,

     LAX_FLOAT64(JSON_QUERY(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[7].value.numeric')) as usd_spent

   from `mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_update_history_creates`

   where template_id_entity_name = 'MemberTraffic'

 ),

 -- We are interested in burn purchased for the target nodes.

 filtered_burn as (

   select * from burn

   where member_id in unnest(validator_parties)

     -- `MemberTraffic` gets combined by the SVs. num_purchases = 1 means actual purchases.

     and burn.num_purchases = 1

     and bucket >= bucket_origin_timestamp

 ),

 grouped_burn as (

   select bucket, sum(traffic) as traffic, sum (amulet_spent) as amulet_spent, sum(usd_spent) as usd_spent

   from filtered_burn

   group by bucket

 ),

 -- Next we count markers.

 markers as (

   select

     update_id,

     record_time,

     TIMESTAMP_BUCKET(timestamp_micros(record_time), bucket_width, bucket_origin_timestamp) as bucket,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[0].value.party') as dso,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[1].value.party') as provider,

     JSON_VALUE(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[2].value.party') as beneficiary,

     LAX_FLOAT64(JSON_QUERY(create_arguments, '$.record.fields[3].value.numeric')) as weight,

   from `mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_update_history_creates`

   where template_id_entity_name = 'FeaturedAppActivityMarker'

 ),

-- We are interested in markers that are either set by the providers OR

-- for the beneficiaries.

-- The former covers an app distributing rewards to others.

-- The latter covers another app's automation writing markers.

 filtered_markers as (

   select * from markers

   where (provider in unnest(provider_parties) or beneficiary in unnest(beneficiary_parties))

     and bucket >= bucket_origin_timestamp

 ),

 grouped_markers as (

   select bucket, sum(weight) as marker_weight

   from filtered_markers

   group by bucket

 ),

-- Lasly we look at CC transfers with the providers as total app weight (markers + CC transfers)

-- should not exceed burn.

transfers AS (

 SELECT

   TIMESTAMP_BUCKET(timestamp_micros(record_time), bucket_width, bucket_origin_timestamp) as bucket,

   JSON_VALUE(argument, '$.record.fields[0].value.record.fields[1].value.party') AS provider

 FROM `mainnet_da2_scan.scan_sv_1_update_history_exercises`

 WHERE choice = 'AmuletRules_Transfer'

),

filtered_transfers AS (

 SELECT *

 FROM transfers

 WHERE bucket >= bucket_origin_timestamp

   AND provider IN UNNEST(provider_parties)

),

grouped_transfers AS (

 SELECT bucket, COUNT(*) as cc_transfers

 FROM filtered_transfers

 GROUP BY bucket

)

-- Lastly, put it all together.

SELECT

 bucket,

 -- Determine type heuristically by deciding which overage is lower.

 if (usd_spent > third_party_submitted, "App", "Asset") as guidance_type,

 marker_weight + cc_transfers as total_app_weight,

 (marker_weight + cc_transfers) / GREATEST(usd_spent, third_party_submitted) as overage,

 * -- optional extra info

FROM grouped_verdicts

 left join grouped_burn using (bucket)

 left join grouped_markers using (bucket)

 left join grouped_transfers using (bucket)

ORDER BY grouped_verdicts.bucket desc

Methodology for Compliant Marker Setting

This section represents a recommendation for how to automate the setting of markers to stay compliant with the guidelines presented.

  1. Burn little and often.
  1. Adjust your traffic purchasing automation to burn CC around once every 10 minutes.
  2. The default topup parameter minTopupInterval: "1m" should ensure this for any app with significant activity.
  3. This allows you to set markers based on burn as opposed to traffic use as both occur within the same 20-30 minute window stipulated by rule 5.
  1. Set markers in batches after the fact.
  1. This maximizes efficiency of setting markers and and thus maximizes profitability when complying with rule 3 (no markers for setting markers)
  2. With BatchV2 coming late March, the efficiency will be at a level where the cost of setting markers can be ignored if this recommendation is followed.
  1. Set markers every round. Ie trigger batches every 5-10 minutes.
  2. Set markers to be compliant for a backwards looking window of 30 minutes.
  1. This ensures compliance with rule 5.
  2. The reference query above to calculate the allowed number of markers.

What the above means practically is every 5 minutes trigger marker automation. The automation performs these steps:

  1. Run the above query for the preceding 30 minutes.
  2. Calculate the number of markers that would result in marker/burn ratio of 1.0 as defined by `overage` in the query.
  3. Set that exact number of markers though the Batch / BatchV2 marker creation contracts.

If this methodology is followed, apps will be and remain compliant with these guidelines no matter the type of activity performed*.

*Note that if featured Canton Coin transfers dominate an app’s activity it is theoretically possible that overage > 1.0 despite the automation and apps’ workflows never setting a single marker.
